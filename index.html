Context

Where the Dream Comes From

The Unawakened Crown sits at the crossing of three traditions. From encounter-driven adventure games like Tales of the Arabian Nights and Talisman it takes the storybook event deck — strange figures met on the road, each demanding a choice before you may pass. From take-that card games in the spirit of Munchkin it takes direct interference: your rivals' Memories can wound you, confuse you, or knock you back down the path. And from modern path-building roguelikes such as Slay the Spire it borrows the idea that the road itself is a deck — revealed card by card, scouted, rearranged, and feared. What none of those games ask, and this one does, is what it costs to move at all: here every advance is paid for by discarding pieces of the path behind you, so the race to wake is also an act of forgetting.

Design

Three Pillars

✦
What

A Race Made of Cards

A shared path deck is the board: dreamers advance by discarding it, scout it, and reshape it, until someone reaches the crown at its end.

☾
How

Memory as Currency

Every turn spends Attention and Memories from your hand — dice-driven events and rival sabotage force you to choose which parts of yourself to burn for speed.

♛
Why

The Cost of Waking

We want players to feel the bittersweet weight of forgetting — the dreamer who sprints to the crown arrives with the least of themselves left to wear it.

Storyworld

The Dream That Would Not End

In The Unawakened Crown, players take the roles of Echoes — ideas, memories, desires, and beliefs that exist within the human mind. Inside a collapsing dream kingdom, these thoughts take physical form and begin to influence one another. The crown that once maintained balance within the dream has lost its power, causing memories to fade and conflicting ideas to struggle for control.

Each Echo follows its own path toward awakening, but no thought exists in isolation. Their choices can strengthen, weaken, reshape, or erase the other Echoes around them. Some may cooperate to restore balance, while others may betray the group to become the dominant truth within the dream. The final ending depends on which ideas survive and how they transform the consciousness that created them.

Process

Playtesting Log

The digital prototype records every playtest automatically — 27 full sessions of telemetry (every card played, event answered, and point of damage dealt) shaped the rules you see today. Three findings changed the game the most:

Going first was worth too much. Across 27 games the first seat won 52% of the time against the second seat’s 37%. We saw it in the damage race: both seats take ~9 damage per game, but seat one converts tempo into wins. Later-seat dreamers now start with compensating draws, and we continue to tune this.
Nobody chose the children — or the elder. In the Cart Dilemma event, 97% of players attacked the merchant rather than pick either sacrifice, making two of three options dead content. Rather than force the sadistic choice, we leaned in: attacking the merchant is now costed as the “heroic tax” option, and the sacrifices were rebalanced to be genuinely tempting shortcuts.
Players ignored what they couldn’t afford. Forgetting (61% ignore rate) and Boundary Disturbance (69%) were skipped whenever Attention ran dry, stalling the events that carry the game’s theme. We lowered their Attention costs and made resolution rewards more visible, pulling the global event resolve rate up toward the 60% we observe now.

Simulation Lab

4,000 Games Nobody Had to Play

To test the rules beyond what 27 human sessions can cover, one of our team built a balance lab: a headless simulator that drives the real game engine (read-only, same public calls as the UI) with three scripted player archetypes — Aggressive hunts low-HP rivals, Defensive heals and hoards, Random plays legal chaos. Each 2,000-game batch seats one Aggressive, one Defensive, and two Random dreamers and lets the dice fall where they may.

Turtling is overtuned. Defensive bots win 98% of baseline games while dealing under 1 HP of damage per game — pure survival is currently the dominant strategy, which tells us the healing economy and defensive Memories need a cost pass before the next revision.
Aggression is a trap — by the numbers. Winning a kill barely moves the odds (80.8% with a kill vs 79.6% without in baseline), only ~30% of attack attempts land damage, and aggressive bots die half the time. Combat needs either cheaper attacks or richer kill rewards to earn its risk.
What the simulator can’t see. The bots never use the three unique items (Belief, Courage, Hope) or initiate trades — those flow through interactive UIs the lab doesn’t script — and it models most defenses as proactive statuses. Those gaps are exactly what the 27 human sessions cover; the two evidence streams are complementary, not redundant.

Emergence

Dynamics & Dilemmas

Mechanics are what we designed; dynamics are what players actually did with them. Across human playtests and simulation, three archetypes kept re-emerging — and every one of them was wrestling with the same four dilemmas.

⇢
Archetype

The Path Rusher

The most common winner: skip the fights, burn the path deck, take the ordinary ending before anyone can interfere. Aggression at the table faded once players discovered speed was safer than swords.

⚔
Archetype

The Conqueror

Some players commit to “defeat everyone” regardless of efficiency — and the data shows they usually finish last. Combat exists to threaten, not to carry a whole strategy.

☍
Archetype

The Strategic Partner

Rare but real: pre-aligned pairs who keep the peace and chase the shared true ending together. Diplomacy stays niche because the true ending’s symbol cards come by event chance, not by treaty.

Progression vs. survival. Every Move costs Attention, so sprinting through the path deck spends the very resource that pays for Memories and event choices — advance too fast and dangerous events catch you unprepared; too slow and rivals catch up.
Spend Attention vs. save it. Attention buys movement, card effects, and event options from one small pool. Early momentum or late flexibility — never both.
Attack vs. advance. Eliminating a rival pays (killers showed a modest win-rate edge in balance testing), but every turn spent fighting is a turn not spent on your own path.
True ending vs. safe rewards. The three symbol Memories — Courage, Hope, Belief — come from the risky, expensive event options: paying HP, max HP, or Attention where a safer choice offered healing. Chasing the crown whole means bleeding for it. One surprise from playtests: deliberate HP sacrifice was often correct — players who hurt now to position for an ending beat players who stayed comfortable.
